I haven't slept a normal night in over four years. And somehow, I feel better than I used to.

When I started [Rootly](https://rootly.com), my sleep schedule didn't just shift — it shattered. Early mornings for US customers, late nights to ship features, a brain that wouldn't stop planning in between. I wasn't pulling all-nighters out of hustle culture bravado. I was just building a company across time zones, and my body started adapting on its own.

At some point I realized I wasn't sleeping badly. I was sleeping *differently*.

## The pattern that emerged

It started without a name. I'd crash hard around 1 AM, wake up naturally around 5 or 6 AM, power through the morning, and then hit a wall in the early afternoon. Instead of fighting it with coffee, I started giving in — a solid 2-to-3-hour nap in the early afternoon. I'd wake up sharp, and suddenly I had a second wind that carried me through until midnight or later.

I wasn't planning this. It just kept happening. My body found a rhythm that my calendar hadn't designed.

One day I looked it up. Turns out there's a name for this: biphasic sleep. A core sleep period at night, plus a dedicated nap during the day. There is [historical evidence](https://www.cambridge.org/core/product/B70D0BFF8E77CFB81A839E9B72240CF2/core-reader) that segmented sleep existed in some pre-industrial contexts, but not in a neat, universal "everyone used to sleep this way" sense. The bigger point for me was simpler: human sleep seems more flexible than the one-perfect-eight-hours story suggests.

## Two streaks of eight hours

Here's what my typical day looks like now:

- **1:00 AM – 5:30 AM** → Core sleep (~4.5 hours)
- **5:30 AM – 1:30 PM** → First work streak (8 hours)
- **1:30 PM – 3:30 PM** → Nap (~2–3 hours)
- **3:30 PM – 11:30 PM** → Second work streak (8 hours)
- **11:30 PM – 1:00 AM** → Wind down, reading, light tasks

Total sleep: ~7–7.5 hours, which still lands in the range [mainstream adult sleep guidance](https://aasm.org/seven-or-more-hours-of-sleep-per-night-a-health-necessity-for-adults/) recommends. Total working hours: ~16. What makes it feel sustainable for me isn't sleeping less. It's getting two clean starts in one day.

When you're building a startup, that second morning feels like a superpower. The afternoon slump that most people fight through with their third coffee? I sleep through it and come out the other side refreshed. By 3:30 PM, while most people are counting down to 5, I'm starting a fresh work block.

## Why it works (for me)

I want to be careful here. I'm not prescribing this. Sleep science is complicated, individual variation is real, and there's a meaningful difference between "I sleep 7 hours and feel great" and "I sleep 7 hours and I'm slowly accumulating a deficit I can't feel."

That said, a few things make me think this rhythm fits my life better than forcing everything into one overnight block:

**I wake up without alarms.** Both from the core sleep and the nap. If I needed to force myself awake, I'd take that as a sign something is off.

**The crash is a feature, not a bug.** Around 1 PM, my energy tanks hard. That used to be a problem — now it's just the signal that it's nap time. And here's the strange part: I sleep *better* during the afternoon nap than I do at night. I wake up from those 2–3 hours feeling more rested than after my core sleep. I don't fully understand why, but it's consistent enough that I've stopped fighting it.

That said, I don't assume that would be a good sign for everyone. For plenty of people, needing long daytime naps can point to sleep debt or [another sleep issue](https://medlineplus.gov/sleepdisorders.html). Research on napping is mixed and highly context-dependent, but reviews do suggest that nap timing and duration matter a lot for whether a nap feels restorative or leaves you groggy with sleep inertia ([review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19645971/), [meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8507757/)).

**It's been over four years.** This isn't a sprint. That still doesn't prove it's optimal. People can adapt subjectively to restricted sleep and still show objective deficits ([Van Dongen et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12683469/)). All I can honestly say is that I haven't noticed the obvious warning signs in my own life — degraded focus, getting sick more often, mood swings — and I treat that as something to keep monitoring, not as proof.

**Weekends self-correct.** If my body needs more sleep, I just sleep longer. The nap extends, or I sleep until 7 AM instead of 5:30. I don't rigidly enforce the schedule — I let my body vote.

I don't think this would have happened if I were working a 9-to-5. But most people assume startup founders sacrifice sleep — sometimes your body finds an adaptation that actually fits the work. The key is paying attention to whether it's working *for* you or *against* you.

## The tradeoff I don't talk about enough

Here's the part that doesn't make it into productivity threads: this schedule is terrible for a social life.

Weekday dinners? Basically off the table — 7 PM is the middle of my second work streak. Grabbing drinks after work? My "after work" is 11:30 PM. Friends learn pretty quickly that you're either unavailable or weird about timing, and over time the invites get less frequent.

I chose this tradeoff. I'm not sure it's the right one. Building [Rootly](https://rootly.com) is the most important thing in my professional life right now, and two work streaks a day is a rhythm I'm reluctant to give up. But I'd be lying if I said there isn't a cost. Relationships need presence, and presence needs overlapping schedules.

Maybe in a few years I'll look back and wish I'd protected my evenings the way I learned to protect my naps. I don't know yet. For now, I'm making the trade with my eyes open — which is the most honest thing I can say about it.

## Then agentic AI almost broke it

For four years, this system just worked. Then I started using AI coding agents — and the cycle started to crack.

I wrote about this in [a previous post]({{< relref "2026-03-09-one-more-prompt-the-dopamine-trap-of-agentic-coding.md" >}}): agentic coding triggers dopamine loops that make it nearly impossible to stop. And it turns out, biphasic sleep is especially vulnerable to that. The whole system depends on honoring the signals — crashing at 1 AM, napping at 1:30 PM. When your brain is lit up watching an agent refactor a module at 1 AM, you don't feel the crash. You push through. When you're in the middle of a promising prompt chain at 1:30 PM, the nap gets skipped.

It didn't blow up overnight. It was subtle — the core sleep drifted later, the nap got shorter or disappeared, and suddenly I was running on 4 hours with no second sleep. I felt fine in the moment (dopamine will do that), but after a few weeks the cracks showed: worse judgment, shorter patience, that foggy feeling I hadn't had in years.

The fix wasn't complicated: I had to treat the sleep schedule as non-negotiable again. The agent can wait. The prompt will still be there after the nap. It sounds obvious, but when you're deep in a flow state powered by variable reinforcement, "just stop" is harder than it sounds.

I'm mostly back on track now. But it was a reminder that even a rhythm you've run for four years can be disrupted if you stop protecting it.

## What I'd tell someone considering it

Don't force it. Seriously. Every "polyphasic sleep hack" article from the 2010s that tells you to set seven alarms and white-knuckle through the adaptation period is nonsense. If your body doesn't naturally gravitate toward split sleep, you're just sleep-depriving yourself with extra steps.

And if you're routinely needing long daytime naps, don't romanticize that either. It might be a rhythm that works for you. It might also mean sleep debt, fragmented nighttime sleep, or an actual sleep issue worth paying attention to.

But if you're already waking up early, already napping in the afternoon, already noticing that your sleep is splitting on its own — maybe stop fighting it. Give the nap a real slot in your calendar. Protect it. See what happens.

The safer version of this experiment is simple: make room for the nap, see whether it improves your energy without wrecking your nighttime sleep, and back off if it doesn't. The best case is you find an extra morning hiding in your afternoon.

I found mine by accident. Over four years and two mornings a day later, I'm still not going back.
